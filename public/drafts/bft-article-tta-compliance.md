# Ghana's Technology Transfer Compliance Gap: The Costs Multinationals Are Not Accounting For

*By Najm Adams Lambon, Founder, Protocol & Transfer Advisory (ptadvisory.co)*

---

A foreign-owned company has operated in Ghana for six years. Every quarter, it remits management fees, software licensing charges, and technical support costs to its regional headquarters. The amounts are commercially reasonable, transfer-priced correctly under group policy, and approved by the group's tax function. Then a Ghana Revenue Authority audit begins — and the question is not whether the fees are arm's length. The question is whether the Technology Transfer Agreements that authorise those remittances were ever registered with the Ghana Investment Promotion Centre.

For a growing number of multinationals operating in Ghana, the answer is no.

---

## The Obligation Most Country Teams Do Not Know They Have

Under the GIPC Act 865 — Ghana's primary foreign investment legislation — any agreement through which a foreign entity provides technology, software, or technical know-how to a Ghanaian entity, in exchange for fees remitted abroad, must be registered with the Ghana Investment Promotion Centre before those remittances have legal standing. This obligation is established in the Act and given further detail under the Technology Transfer Regulations (L.I. 1547). *(⚠ Verify: confirm L.I. 1547 is the correct citation for the Technology Transfer Regulations under GIPC Act 865 before submitting)*

The obligation applies broadly. It covers software licences, network management agreements, technical service contracts, brand and trademark royalties, and training agreements with a technology transfer component. A single multinational with a Ghana subsidiary may have five or six separate agreements that individually require GIPC registration.

The registration process is not administratively burdensome. A compliant agreement, properly drafted to meet GIPC content requirements, typically registers within ten to fifteen business days. The problem is not the process. The problem is that most country teams in Ghana are never told the obligation exists.

Intercompany fee arrangements are typically designed at group or regional level, by teams that understand transfer pricing and group tax policy but are not necessarily familiar with Ghana's investment registration framework. The country team in Accra inherits a fee structure and begins paying — without any TTA registration in place.

---

## Three Channels Through Which Non-Compliance Becomes Costly

When a TTA compliance gap surfaces — through an audit, a remittance block, or a GIPC inspection — it creates exposure across three distinct channels simultaneously.

| Channel | Exposure | What Triggers It | Resolution Complexity |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Remittance** | All technology-related fee payments made without a registered TTA are legally unprotected. Banks are required to verify TTA registration before processing outward remittances. | Bank compliance review; GRA audit; GIPC inspection | Moderate — registration can be obtained on a go-forward basis, but historic payments remain exposed |
| **Corporate Tax** | Ghana Revenue Authority may disallow technology fees paid to related parties without a registered TTA as non-deductible expenses. This converts an operating cost into taxable income, with interest and penalties on the resulting underpayment. | GRA transfer pricing audit; corporate income tax assessment | High — retrospective tax assessments are difficult and costly to contest |
| **Licence and Standing** | Multinationals in regulated sectors (telecommunications, banking, mining) depend on sector licences with renewal processes that involve assessments of regulatory standing. GIPC non-compliance can introduce friction — and in some cases, conditions — into those renewal processes. | Sector licence renewal (NCA, Bank of Ghana, Minerals Commission) | Variable — depends on the sector regulator and the severity of the GIPC non-compliance |

The compounding effect is significant. A company that has operated for three years with unregistered TTAs may face: disallowed deductions on three years of technology fees; withholding tax recalculation on the same payments; and a GIPC standing issue that surfaces precisely when its sector licence is due for renewal.

---

## Why the Problem Is Structural, Not Accidental

Non-compliance with Ghana's TTA registration requirements is rarely the result of a deliberate decision. It results from a structural disconnect between the level at which intercompany arrangements are designed — typically group or regional — and the level at which local regulatory obligations must be met.

Group tax teams understand transfer pricing. Regional finance teams understand intercompany billing cycles. What is consistently absent from the design of these arrangements is a country-level compliance layer that identifies which fee flows require local registration in each jurisdiction. In a market like Ghana, where the TTA registration obligation is specific, consequential, and not widely understood outside the local practice, that gap has a direct financial cost.

The Ghana country team is rarely positioned to identify this independently. Country Directors and Finance Managers are focused on operational execution, not regulatory design. Legal Counsel, where present locally, often handles commercial and employment matters — not GIPC compliance. The TTA registration obligation falls between functions and is missed.

---

## The GIPA Act 2025 Raises the Stakes

The Ghana Investment Promotion Authority Act 2025 (GIPA Act 2025) introduces the most significant overhaul of Ghana's investment registration framework in over a decade. Its implications for TTA compliance are direct.

The GIPA Act introduces updated content requirements for Technology Transfer Agreements — with particular attention to digital technology, software, and data processing arrangements that were not specifically addressed in the GIPC Act 865 framework. It also introduces revised enforcement provisions and a re-registration obligation for existing TTAs.

For companies that have been operating with non-compliant or unregistered TTAs, this presents two realities simultaneously. The first is increased exposure: the new framework is expected to apply stricter scrutiny during the transition period. The second is an opportunity: the re-registration process creates a defined moment at which non-compliant arrangements can be corrected, restructured, and registered properly before enforcement intensifies.

Companies that use the GIPA transition period to audit and regularise their TTA position will be structurally better placed than those that wait for a formal re-registration deadline. The compliance window is open now. The enforcement rush, when it comes, will be difficult to navigate retrospectively.

---

## The Calculation Is Not Complicated

The cost of TTA registration — legal drafting, compliance review, and GIPC submission — is fixed, modest, and incurred once. The cost of non-compliance is variable, accumulating, and distributed across remittance, tax, and licensing channels simultaneously. For any multinational operating in Ghana with upstream technology fee arrangements, the calculation is not complicated.

What the GIPA Act 2025 changes is the timeline. The compliance gap that has existed quietly for years is now subject to a new regulatory framework with new enforcement mechanisms. Companies that have treated TTA registration as a detail can no longer reasonably do so.
